Keynote Speakers

Dr. Beatriz Vicoso

Sex (and lack thereof) in Artemia brine shrimp

Sexual reproduction is ancestral to animals, but the ways in which sex is determined are astonishingly diverse. Why such diversity evolves, and how some lineages abandon sex altogether, remain open questions. Artemia brine shrimp have emerged as a promising model to tackle some of these questions. Unlike most model systems, which have XY sex chromosomes, Artemia have ZW sex chromosomes. Some lineages have transitioned to asexual reproduction, enabling direct comparisons between closely related sexually and asexually reproducing lineages.

We characterized the ZW pair across the clade and uncovered striking convergent features with well-studied XY systems. These include a regulatory architecture reminiscent of Drosophila melanogaster and evidence for a mechanism analogous to mammalian meiotic sex chromosome inactivation. Crucially, genetic crosses implicate a modified version of the Z chromosome in the transition to asexuality. This chromosome carries many disrupting mutations at a gene essential for meiotic progression, suggesting a candidate mechanism for the rewiring of meiosis for parthenogenesis.

Together, our findings reveal commonalities in sex chromosome biology across deeply divergent systems and provide new insights into the minimal molecular changes required for the evolution of asexual reproduction.

Dr. Sophie Karrenberg

Why are seperate sexes rare in plants?

Different from animals, most plants are hermaphrodites. In angiosperms, 6% of the species have separate sexes (dioecy) — this sexual system has evolved intependently many times, in different lineages. This situation makes plants attractive models to study the evolution of sexual systems and sex chromsomes. Sex chromosomes are being discovered in more and more plants pecies; however, in most dioecious plants, the mechanism of sex determination is still unknown. In my talk, I review the main theories on the causes and consequences of dioecy in plants and introduce empirical work on the evolution of sex ratio bias and sex chromsomes in willows (Salix).
